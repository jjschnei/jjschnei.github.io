Diversity in Tech

December 6th, 2015
Jeff Schneider

Technology jobs are disproportionately filled by whites, Asians and men. While many tech companies have made it a public goal to diversity their workforce, there is still a long ways to go. A recent Fortune survey of 9 top tech companies found that on average women make up about one-third of the work force.

The lack of diversity in tech has often been justified as a pipeline issue: there are more men who study computer science so there are more men in tech jobs. An examination of the numbers reveals that isn’t always the case. For example, 50% of introductory computer science students at Stanford and Berkeley are women. It is not merely a pipeline issue.

Why it Matter

While tech leaders have long pledged their support for diversity, the makeup of tech companies is still lopsided. Creating software is a highly creative enterprise. Equal employment opportunity for everyone is not only a moral obligation, it's also good business. Having a broader and more diverse team will result in better ideas and products.

How We Fix the Issue

It's a step in the right direction that tech companies are starting to track and publish these statistics (what gets measured gets managed). But there is more to be done. I believe that one of the ways to solve this problem is by providing universal early access to computers. It’s critical that public schools have k-12 computer science (and AP computer science) courses. This will make a big impact on future generations. Right now, we need to make sure that the culture and hiring practices at tech companies are inclusive for women and all minority groups.
